## Article Structure (800–1,200 words is optimal for LinkedIn; 1,500–2,500 for journals)

### 1. Opening hook (1–2 paragraphs)
Start with a specific clinical observation, not a generalisation. The opening should feel like the beginning of a case discussion between peers, not a marketing introduction.

**Strong opening examples:**
- "In 26 years of functional medicine practice, the most common documentation error I have seen is not inaccuracy — it is incompleteness at the systems level."
- "The IFM matrix is one of the most powerful clinical thinking tools in functional medicine. It is also, in its current form, one of the most time-consuming to produce properly."
- "Cascade propagation is the concept that most separates terrain medicine from symptom-based practice. It is also the concept most often left implicit in clinical documentation."

**Avoid:**
- Starting with company/product name
- Starting with a statistic you cannot source to T1/T2
- Starting with "I am a functional medicine practitioner" — the audience knows this

---

### 2. Clinical context / the problem (2–3 paragraphs)
Establish the clinical or operational challenge you are addressing. Use specific, precise language. Do not oversimplify for a general audience — this is peer-to-peer content.

*Example themes:*
- The documentation burden in complex, multi-system cases
- The gap between clinical reasoning quality and documentation speed
- How cascade propagation is currently handled (or not handled) in standard documentation
- The structural difference between terrain-based and diagnosis-based clinical frameworks

---

### 3. Your clinical perspective (3–4 paragraphs)
This is where Dr Faisal's 26 years of experience is the primary source. First-person clinical reasoning. This section should feel like a clinical teaching moment — the kind of insight a senior practitioner shares with a colleague.

**Voice notes:**
- Direct. Not hedged to the point of saying nothing.
- Clinically precise. Use correct functional medicine terminology.
- Peer-to-peer. Not authoritative-to-novice.
- Evidence-grounded where possible (T1–T4). Flag where reasoning is from clinical experience (T5).

---

### 4. Practical application (2–3 paragraphs)
What can a functional medicine practitioner reading this actually do differently after reading the article? Make this concrete. The best articles leave the reader with a specific change in clinical thinking or practice.

---

### 5. Close (1 paragraph)
End on a forward-looking observation or an open clinical question — not a call to action. Save the CTA for the comment or the featured link.

**Avoid:** Ending with "...which is why I built VitalMatrix." The article earns the right for readers to find VitalMatrix on their own. The platform can be mentioned naturally in context — not as a closing pitch.

---

### 6. Author bio (always append)
> *Dr Shahzad Faisal MBBS, FAAMFM is a functional medicine practitioner with 26 years of clinical experience and founder of VitalMatrix — a clinical intelligence platform for terrain medicine practitioners.*
