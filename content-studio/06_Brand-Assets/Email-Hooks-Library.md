## What a Hook Is

The hook is the first 1–2 sentences of the email body. It determines whether the email gets read past line 3.

For functional medicine practitioners — a highly educated, time-pressed, scepticism-trained audience — the hook must do one of three things instantly:
1. **Name a clinical reality they live** — they nod and keep reading
2. **Say something they have not seen said** — they are curious enough to continue
3. **Signal peer-to-peer respect** — they do not feel sold to

What a hook must never do: open with a compliment, a question about their wellbeing, a generic health claim, or anything that signals "marketing email." These practitioners delete that category in under two seconds.

## Hook Categories

### TYPE 1 — The Clinical Observation Hook
Opens with a specific, accurate clinical observation from practice. Works because it signals: *this person knows what my work actually looks like.*

**Best for:** N1 (Day 2 nurture), thought leadership articles, post-call Version A

**Formula:** State a specific clinical truth without editorialising.

**Examples:**
> "In functional medicine, the most diagnostically complex patients are rarely complex because of a single node. They are complex because of what is happening between nodes."

> "The documentation bottleneck in terrain-based practice is not writing the plan. It is producing the architecture that the plan depends on."

> "Every IFM-trained practitioner I have spoken to describes the same experience: the clinical reasoning is there before the consultation ends. The documentation takes hours after."

> "Multi-system presentations do not fail on diagnosis. They fail on sequencing — knowing which terrain burden to address first and in what order."

> "The gap between what a functional medicine practitioner knows in the room and what they can document before the next patient is the single biggest bottleneck in the field."

**Avoid:**
- ❌ "Functional medicine is changing healthcare as we know it."
- ❌ "As a fellow clinician, I know how busy your practice is."
- ❌ "Did you know that chronic disease affects millions of people?"

---

### TYPE 2 — The Direct Situation Hook
Opens by describing the practitioner's situation with enough specificity that it feels like Dr Faisal has observed their practice. Not generic — particular.

**Best for:** N2 (Day 4 nurture), discovery call pre-call email, onboarding emails

**Formula:** Describe a specific moment or situation in their working day without naming them.

**Examples:**
> "You have just finished a consultation with a patient presenting across four or five systems. You know what the terrain picture is. Writing it down will take another hour."

> "Before I tell you anything about VitalMatrix, I want to understand how you work."

> "There is a specific moment in a complex case when the clinical picture is clear and the documentation has not started. VitalMatrix exists for that moment."

> "If you are currently building IFM matrices by hand, this email is relevant to you. If you are not, it probably is not."

> "Most of the practitioners I speak to describe the same constraint: the clinical thinking is not the problem. The time to document it properly is."

**Avoid:**
- ❌ "I hope this email finds you well."
- ❌ "You are probably very busy, so I will keep this short."
- ❌ "I wanted to reach out because I think VitalMatrix could help you."

---

### TYPE 3 — The Counterintuitive Hook
Opens with something that contradicts the expected framing. Stops the delete reflex because it is not what the reader anticipated.

**Best for:** Thought leadership articles, LinkedIn posts, conference materials, N3 (Day 6 nurture)

**Formula:** State the opposite of what the reader expects you to say, then resolve it.

**Examples:**
> "VitalMatrix is not for every functional medicine practitioner. Most of the calls I take end with me saying the timing is not right."

> "The founding cohort is 10 practitioners. That is not an early-access limitation. It is the number the architecture requires."

> "I am not interested in a founding practitioner who is not getting value from the platform. If after 30 days it is not working for your practice, contact me directly."

> "The most common question I get is whether VitalMatrix replaces clinical judgement. The answer is no — and it should not. A platform that removed the clinician from the decision would be clinically dangerous and commercially worthless."

> "Functional medicine does not have a thinking problem. It has an infrastructure problem."

**Avoid:**
- ❌ Any hook that is counterintuitive for effect rather than for accuracy
- ❌ Manufactured controversy
- ❌ Negativity about the field or about patients

---

### TYPE 4 — The Personal Story Hook
Dr Faisal's own clinical experience as the opening. Highest trust-building hook type. Requires real, specific stories — not generic "I was a clinician" framing.

**Best for:** N1 (Day 2 nurture — after user provides context), founding narrative emails, LinkedIn About section, thought leadership articles

**Formula:** A specific clinical moment, honestly told. First person. One concrete detail that makes it real.

**Placeholder examples (replace with Dr Faisal's actual stories):**
> "I had a patient who had seen eleven practitioners before she reached me. The notes from each one described different presentations. None of them described the same terrain."

> "For 26 years I built these matrices by hand. For 26 years the clinical reasoning was faster than the documentation. I assumed that was just how it was."

> "There was a point in my practice where I was spending more time on the architecture of a case than on the conversation with the patient. That is when I knew the infrastructure had to change."

**Note:** These placeholders are structurally correct but need Dr Faisal's real clinical stories to reach their potential. When he provides context, these become the highest-converting hooks in the library.

---

### TYPE 5 — The Factual Anchor Hook
Opens with a specific, verifiable or proprietary fact. Creates instant credibility through precision rather than claims.

**Best for:** N2 (Day 4 nurture), website homepage, investor materials, conference introductions

**Formula:** One specific number or fact, stated without commentary, that forces the reader to contextualise it.

**Examples:**
> "26 years of functional medicine practice. For most of that time, I built IFM matrices by hand."

> "5,000+ terrain computation algorithms. One clinical document. Under 60 seconds."

> "Ten founding practitioner slots. Not eleven. Not twenty. Ten."

> "The NCZ™ framework maps seven biological nodes across five terrain zones. It was not designed in a lab. It was developed over two decades of clinical practice."

> "VitalMatrix Ltd. Company No. 17046123. ICO ZC101813. Founded by a practitioner, not a software company."

**Avoid:**
- ❌ Statistics you cannot source (evidence tier check required — see Section 21)
- ❌ Superlatives ("the most advanced", "the only platform")
- ❌ Rounded numbers that read as marketing ("100s of algorithms")

---

### TYPE 6 — The Soft Close Hook (final-stage emails)
Opens by acknowledging the practitioner's position without pressure. Used only in final-stage emails (N4, post-call Version B follow-ups, Phase 1 close-date emails).

**Best for:** N4 (Day 8 nurture), PATH B follow-up emails, L3 (launch sequence close)

**Formula:** Acknowledge their situation directly. Remove pressure. Make the ask once, quietly.

**Examples:**
> "I know how busy clinical practice is. I have been in it for 26 years."

> "If now is not the right time, that is completely fine. I would rather you reach out when the moment is right than feel any pressure from this email."

> "This is a short note. Phase 1 closes on [DATE]. After that, the founding cohort is complete."

> "You have had a few emails from me over the past week. This is the last one before the discovery call window closes."

> "No follow-up after this one. If it is right for your practice, the link below goes straight to my calendar."

**Critical rule:** TYPE 6 hooks must never be used before N4/final stage. Used too early, they read as resignation. Used at the right moment, they read as respect.

| N2 — Day 4 nurture | TYPE 2 or TYPE 5 | Situational — describe their clinical reality; or factual anchor |
| N3 — Day 6 nurture | TYPE 3 or TYPE 5 | Counterintuitive re: founding cohort; or factual anchor on terms |

## Subject Line Formulas

Subject lines are read before hooks. They determine open rate. VitalMatrix subject lines follow one of four formulas:

| Formula | Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **The Direct Statement** | State what the email is about | "Phase 1 is open — 10 founding slots" |
| **The Specific Question** | One question that the email answers | "What does VitalMatrix actually do in the consulting room?" |
| **The Situation Frame** | Describe their situation briefly | "Your VitalMatrix discovery call — tomorrow at [TIME]" |
| **The Quiet Update** | Factual, no hype | "Week 1 — how has VitalMatrix been?" |

**Subject line rules:**
- Never use emojis in subject lines (practitioner audience — reads as unprofessional)
- Never use ALL CAPS
- Never use punctuation for urgency (!!!, ???)
- Never use "RE:" as a false reply thread
- Maximum 60 characters for mobile preview
- Always test: would a functional medicine consultant open this?

## Preview Text (pre-header)

The preview text appears after the subject line in most email clients. It should extend the subject line's logic, not repeat it.

| Subject | Preview text |
|---------|-------------|
| "Phase 1 is open — 10 founding slots" | "This is not a figure we arrived at for marketing purposes." |
| "A bit more about Dr Faisal — VitalMatrix" | "26 years. IFM. Harley Street. Why I built this." |
| "What VitalMatrix does in the consulting room" | "From blank matrix to completed architecture — in one session." |
| "Is the discovery call still on your list?" | "30 minutes. Directly with me. No sales team." |

**Rule:** Preview text should make the reader more curious, not summarise what they are about to read.
